1. Water Service Coverage by Sector
Key Finding: Coverage ranges from 29% (Muyumbu) to 100% (Kigabiro & Munyaga). Six sectors remain critically underserved with coverage below 50%: Muyumbu (29%), Nyakaliro (37%), Nzige (38%), Karenge (41%), Muhazi (44%) and Gahengeri (47%). These sectors serve as priority targets for infrastructure investment.
Reading this chart: Bars are colour-coded by coverage tier — red (<40%), amber (40–49%), teal (50–74%), green (≥75%). The dotted line marks the 100% national target. Muyumbu records the lowest coverage at 29%, meaning nearly 3 in 4 residents have no access. Kigabiro and Munyaga achieve full 100% coverage. Six sectors remain critically below 50% — these are the district's highest-priority investment zones under the NST1 2017–2024 framework.
Reading this chart: Each bar represents the total sector population split into served (teal) and unserved (red) residents. Kigabiro has the largest served population (38,865 — 100% of its 38,865 residents). Muyumbu, despite having 32,755 people, leaves 23,256 unserved — the highest absolute unserved count in the district. Combined, the six critically underserved sectors account for approximately 82,000 people still lacking safe water access.
2. Public Water Infrastructure
Key Finding: Munyaga has the highest density of public water points (80), supporting 100% coverage, while Muyumbu and Nzige each have only 12 water points despite large populations — a critical infrastructure gap requiring urgent attention.
Reading this chart: Munyaga leads with 80 public water points, directly supporting its 100% coverage. Muyumbu and Nzige each have only 12 points — the lowest in the district — despite serving large populations. Fumbwe and Rubona have 45 and 51 points respectively, correlating with their above-average 73% and 76% coverage rates. This distribution confirms that physical infrastructure density is a primary determinant of service coverage.
Reading this chart: Each bubble represents a sector; bubble size scales with the number of public water points; colour indicates coverage tier. Kigabiro stands out with 5,411 household connections and 100% coverage. Most sectors cluster at low HH connections (<500) with mid-range coverage, suggesting communal standpipes dominate over household connections. Karenge (2,768 HH connections, 41% coverage) is an outlier — indicating that HH connections alone do not guarantee population-wide coverage.
Reading this chart: Network pipe length (km) does not directly predict coverage. Muyumbu has the longest network (1,231 km) yet the lowest coverage (29%), indicating that existing pipes are poorly distributed, ageing, or non-functional. Conversely, Munyaga achieves 100% coverage with just 81 km — demonstrating that strategic network design and maintenance quality outweigh raw infrastructure quantity.
3. Water Reliability & Quantity
Reliability Crisis: 88% of improved water sources supply water for only 1–2 days per week, far below the national target of 24 hours per day. Only 5% of households (those using boreholes and springs) enjoy a reliable 24-hour supply.
Quantity: The mean consumption of 29 L/capita/day exceeds the 20 L/c/d national minimum. However, 40% of households remain below this benchmark due to affordability constraints, unreliable supply, and physical access barriers.
Reading this gauge: The needle shows mean household water consumption at 29 L/capita/day, exceeding Rwanda's national minimum of 20 L/c/d (red threshold line). The green zone (20–29 L/c/d) confirms the district average is above the minimum. However, this average masks deep inequality — households in underserved sectors with unreliable supply consume far less, while well-connected urban households skew the mean upward.
Reading this chart: 60% of surveyed households (n=150) meet or exceed the 20 L/capita/day national water target, while 40% remain below it. The below-target group is concentrated in sectors with intermittent supply and high diesel-pumping tariffs, where affordability and physical access barriers limit daily consumption despite infrastructure presence.
Reading this chart: An alarming 88% of improved water sources supply water for only 1–2 days per week — far below the RURA-mandated 24-hour daily supply. Only 5% (boreholes and protected springs) offer reliable daily access. Chronic intermittency forces households to queue, store water in unsafe containers, or fall back on unimproved sources, compounding health risks and time burdens, particularly for women and children.
Reading this chart: Only 47.5% of surveyed households expressed satisfaction with water quantity and service. 37.5% are dissatisfied — directly reflecting the widespread supply intermittency. The 15% neutral response likely represents households with seasonal variation in satisfaction, satisfied during rainy season but struggling during dry months when supply pressure drops further.
4. Water Affordability & Tariffs
Affordability: 75% of surveyed households paid for water. Diesel-pumped rural water costs 1,087 RWF/m³ (incl. VAT) — over 3× the gravity rate (338 RWF/m³) — creating significant inequity between well-served and remote areas.
Reading this chart: WASAC's urban piped water tariff structure (VAT exclusive) is progressive. Public taps have the lowest flat rate (323 RWF/m³), promoting equitable access for the poorest households. Residential tariffs increase from 340 RWF/m³ (0–5 m³) to 877 RWF/m³ (>50 m³), discouraging wasteful consumption. Industrial consumers pay a flat 736 RWF/m³. This progressive structure rewards conservation and cross-subsidises low-income users.
Reading this chart: Rural water costs vary sharply by system type. Gravity-fed systems — used in accessible terrain — are the most affordable at 338 RWF/m³ (incl. VAT). Diesel-pumped systems, common in remote areas, cost 1,087 RWF/m³ — over 3× the gravity rate. This creates a perverse pricing structure where the poorest, most remote communities pay the highest prices for water. Targeted subsidies for diesel-system users are a key policy recommendation.
5. Key Conclusions & Recommendations
Access Typology: 29.1% of households access public standpipes within the optimal 500 m / 30-minute threshold; 27.3% must travel further. Physical proximity alone is insufficient — reliability and affordability are equally critical.
Priority Sectors: Muyumbu, Nyakaliro, Nzige, Karenge, Muhazi and Gahengeri require immediate infrastructure expansion and network rehabilitation to meet national NST1 2017–2024 targets.
Reliability Gap: RURA must enforce the 24-hour supply mandate. MININFRA should prioritise rehabilitation of WASAC-managed systems where intermittent supply affects the majority of connected households.
Equity in Pricing: Progressive tariff reform and targeted subsidies are recommended to reduce the cost differential between gravity and diesel-pumped networks, which disproportionately burden remote rural communities.
